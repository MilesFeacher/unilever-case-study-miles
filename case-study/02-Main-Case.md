## Main Case Narrative

### 1. The Monday Morning Question

On a cold February morning in 2026, Sam Kini opened her laptop in London and stared at three windows that did not naturally belong together.

The first was a finance summary from the prior year: EUR 50.5 billion in turnover, down 3.8%. Underlying operating profit of EUR 10.1 billion, down 1.1%. Free cash flow at EUR 5.9 billion. Cash conversion at 100%. Shareholder returns at EUR 6.0 billion.

The second was a strategy memo about AI and commerce. The language was ambitious and directional: Unilever needed to become "fit for the AI Age," move faster on demand generation, and work with retailers and large language model ecosystems as shopping behavior evolved.

The third was a list of projects already under way across business groups and regions: marketing content workflows, data integration workstreams, supply chain optimization pilots, and platform decisions that looked manageable one by one but risky in aggregate.

The question in front of Kini, Unilever's Chief Digital and Technology Officer, was not whether the company should embrace AI. That argument had already been won in principle. The harder question was sequencing in practice.

Where should Unilever push hard first, with enough scale to matter and enough control to avoid enterprise fragmentation?

If she optimized for speed, she could produce visible commercial wins and support leadership's growth narrative. If she optimized for architecture and governance first, she could reduce long-term risk but appear slow in a market where competitors announced new AI moves almost monthly. If she tried to do both everywhere at once, she could produce the most common failure mode in large transformations: many successful pilots, no shared operating system.

The steering discussion later that week would force a choice. Her recommendation would shape where capital, talent, and management attention went first.

### 2. A Leader Built For Change, But Not For Illusion

Kini's leadership style had been shaped long before generative AI entered boardroom vocabulary. In a widely viewed 2022 interview, she described Unilever's mission through purpose, sustainability, and long-term value creation. She emphasized two themes that became central to her approach in 2025-2026.

First, she framed transformation as cultural and behavioral, not purely technical. Teams needed psychological safety, practical upskilling, and willingness to adapt. "Learning to cope with change," she argued, was a strategic capability. In the same interview, she added that leaders had to "create an environment... where people can speak up without fear of retribution."

Second, she treated enterprise technology as a simplification exercise. She highlighted central technology leadership, shared platforms, and reduction of unnecessary complexity. In earlier transformation phases, she pointed to cloud migration progress and SAP consolidation as examples of how discipline at infrastructure level could unlock strategic flexibility later.

That posture gave her credibility internally. But by early 2026, her role had changed. The challenge was no longer mainly about digitizing core processes. It was about designing an AI-enabled operating system that could reliably connect brand building, market sensing, demand generation, and fulfillment.

### 3. Financial Strength, Strategic Pressure

Unilever entered 2026 with a profile that made strategic choices harder, not easier.

The company was financially resilient. Cash generation was strong. Capital allocation looked disciplined. The balance sheet could support investment. In the annual report and earnings call cycle, management reaffirmed a major productivity program, stating that it had delivered more than EUR 670 million of savings and remained on track to complete EUR 800 million in 2026.

Yet resilience did not remove pressure.

Turnover decline signaled market friction. Consumer demand remained uneven in many markets. Growth now depended on sharper mix, stronger execution, and faster adaptation in channels increasingly influenced by algorithmic discovery and recommendation systems.

Performance was strongest where strategy and execution were tightly coupled. In 2025, Beauty and Wellbeing grew 4.3% and Personal Care 4.7% in underlying sales growth, while Home Care grew 1.5%. On profitability, management disclosed strong segment margin levels, including 19.2% for Beauty and Wellbeing and 22.6% for Personal Care.

To leadership, these numbers suggested that disciplined reinvestment and portfolio focus were working. To Kini, they suggested something more specific: the next wave of value would come from cross-functional execution speed and quality. AI would matter only if it improved decision velocity, conversion quality, and operating consistency.

### 4. Leadership Context: Rewiring At The Top

The operating context around Kini was shifting at senior levels.

CEO Fernando Fernandez was explicit about priorities: volume growth, positive mix, gross margin expansion, and profit growth in hard currency. He repeatedly stressed execution quality and premiumization while pushing the organization toward simplification and sharper focus.

At the same time, marketing leadership changed. Annual report disclosures confirmed that Esi Eggleston Bracey stood down effective 31 December 2025 and left the company on 31 January 2026. Leandro Barreto was appointed Chief Marketing Officer for Unilever and Beauty and Wellbeing.

Barreto's first major public framing in March 2026 described modern marketing as a balance of "poetry and plumbing" - creative relevance plus systems rigor. For Kini, this was more than a communications phrase. It mapped to the technology reality she faced: AI-enabled marketing required not only better content generation, but integrated data, reliable workflows, governance standards, and decision rights that crossed functions.

Leadership alignment looked promising. But alignment in message did not automatically create alignment in incentives, funding cadence, or process discipline across regions and categories.

### 5. Evidence The Company Could Execute

Unilever was not starting from scratch. In several places, execution was already visible and measurable.

In operations and supply chain, Unilever highlighted outcomes from sites recognized in the World Economic Forum's Global Lighthouse Network updates in 2026.

In Hefei, China, a factory-to-consumer distribution model reportedly combined high automation and digitally enabled use cases. Published outcomes included 75% faster fulfillment, 24% lower logistics costs, and the ability to handle more than 31,000 orders per day across 400+ products, with about 90% automation and approximately 120 robots.

In Gandhidham, India, digital-twin and AI-enabled optimization was associated with 17% lower on-site water use, 48% less material waste, and 90% reduction in Scope 1 and 2 emissions, while supporting production growth.

In Pondicherry, India, machine-learning quality controls and digital production tools were associated with doubled production speed, a 25% volume increase, and expanded product variants without new physical footprint.

On the commercial side, the annual report said Unilever was "embedding AI-powered design capabilities into brand teams via Sketch Pro - our in-house studio." That line mattered because it showed the company was moving AI inside day-to-day brand work, not keeping it at the level of experimentation alone.

Taken together, these examples mattered for board-level confidence. They demonstrated that AI at Unilever could move beyond pilots into operational outcomes. But they also exposed a strategic tension: these were strong nodes, not yet one enterprise-wide operating model.

### 6. The Market Was Moving Faster Than Internal Comfort

External commentary raised the stakes.

Industry publications in February 2026 described Unilever's Google Cloud partnership as an "agentic AI" and "agentic commerce" move designed to support marketing transformation, integrated data platforms, and faster demand generation. Coverage in CIO Dive and Marketing Dive framed the deal as a step toward AI-first operating capabilities rather than a narrow IT modernization exercise.

The company itself used sharper language in the joint announcement. Willem Uijen said, "Technology has moved to the core of value creation at Unilever," and Tara Brady of Google Cloud added that the partnership would "create a system of intelligence that reasons, learns, and acts." The same release said the collaboration would help Unilever build "an enterprise-wide, AI-first digital backbone" and support "agentic workflows."

Cloud Wars commentary emphasized the same direction with a different tone: this was a shift in how brands would be discovered and shopped in increasingly conversational, model-mediated consumer journeys.

From one angle, this external framing helped Unilever by signaling strategic ambition to investors, partners, and talent.

From another angle, it increased delivery risk. Public narrative moved quickly toward expectations of enterprise-scale outcomes. Internally, however, much of the work remained in integration details that rarely made headlines: data-model consistency, interoperability, role redesign, governance thresholds, and accountability for measurable value.

Kini understood that gap. She had seen versions of it before. Once a transformation became a headline, business units often interpreted urgency as permission to move in parallel directions. Without clear architecture, clear incentives, and hard gates for scale, speed could become expensive noise. The release language itself was ambitious, but the operating challenge was still practical: turn a "system of intelligence" into a system people could actually use.

### 7. What Exactly Was The Decision?

By late February 2026, Kini and her leadership peers were converging on three pathways.

Pathway A was commercialization-first.
Unilever could concentrate resources on high-visibility use cases in marketing and digital commerce, aiming for fast wins in discovery, personalization, and campaign-to-conversion speed. This approach would support growth narratives and build momentum. The risk was technical debt and uneven enterprise capability if foundational integration lagged.

Pathway B was platform-first.
The company could prioritize enterprise data and workflow integration, building common standards before scaling use cases aggressively. This approach would reduce long-run fragmentation risk and support stronger governance. The risk was slower visible impact, especially in markets where competitors moved faster in consumer-facing AI execution.

Pathway C was federated dual-track.
Unilever could run a central platform track and a managed portfolio of business-led use cases in parallel, with strict stage gates for scaling and explicit stop rules for underperforming pilots. This approach balanced speed and control but required unusually strong coordination and governance discipline.

None of the options was obviously superior. The choice depended on constraints that were partly technical, partly managerial, and partly political.

### 8. Strategic Tensions Without Clean Answers

As Kini and colleagues debated the path forward, at least five tensions remained unresolved.

Speed versus coherence.
How much architectural imperfection could Unilever tolerate to move faster in market-facing use cases?

Central control versus business ownership.
Should AI capability design be centrally standardized, or should business groups own more of the stack to preserve speed and category-specific learning?

Efficiency versus differentiation.
Where should Unilever rely on partner capabilities and standardized cloud services, and where should it build proprietary assets that could become long-term competitive advantage?

Short-term proof versus long-term resilience.
How should management weigh quick demand-generation wins against slower but more durable capability building in data quality, model governance, and workforce redesign?

Narrative versus evidence.
How should leadership communicate ambition externally without over-claiming operational maturity internally?

The final tension was subtle but critical. Unilever's own evidence showed success where execution discipline was high. But scaling discipline across a global organization often required decisions that felt locally constraining. In other words, the governance needed for scale could reduce local autonomy in the near term.

### 9. The Moment Before Commitment

By spring 2026, the pieces were on the table.

The company had cash flow and capital discipline.
Leadership had articulated strategic intent around AI and growth.
Operations had demonstrated credible digital outcomes in multiple regions.
Marketing leadership had repositioned around a creativity-plus-systems model.
External analysts and trade media had raised expectations around agentic commerce and AI-led transformation.

Kini was preparing recommendations for execution priorities and governance architecture. The plan needed to satisfy multiple audiences at once: the CEO and board seeking growth confidence, commercial leaders seeking speed, operations leaders seeking reliability, and risk owners seeking control.

Her team could deliver a sequence in weeks. The consequences of that sequence would play out over years.

If they moved fast and fragmented, Unilever might get near-term wins but lose enterprise leverage.
If they moved slowly and cleanly, they might build stronger foundations but miss market timing in digitally mediated discovery.
If they tried to do everything, they risked exhausting the organization without proving value clearly enough.

In her notes for the next steering meeting, Kini wrote a final line under the heading "Decision Criteria":

"Do not optimize for activity. Optimize for repeatable value at scale."

The meeting invite was already on her calendar.

She still had to decide what that meant first. The question was no longer whether AI mattered; it was how much complexity the organization could absorb while learning fast enough to matter.

## Instructor Cold-Call Opener

You are Sam Kini. The steering meeting starts in 20 minutes. You can recommend only one first move:
- a commercialization-first sprint,
- a platform-first consolidation,
- or a federated dual-track model with strict stage gates.

What do you recommend, what metric will define success in 12 months, and what risk are you accepting by making that choice?

## Discussion Questions

1. You get one move first. Which path do you pick - commercialization-first, platform-first, or federated dual-track - and what is your explicit rationale for sequencing?

2. If the board asks for one headline KPI in 12 months, what should it be: growth lift, margin impact, cycle-time reduction, or capability maturity? What crucial tradeoff does your KPI choice hide?

3. Which three decisions must be centralized now (for example data standards, model governance, vendor architecture), and which two decisions should stay in business units to preserve speed?

4. How should leadership communicate agentic-commerce ambition externally without over-claiming operational maturity? Where is the line between signaling and overselling?

5. In a company this large, where should the boundary sit between partner-enabled and proprietary capability? Which capabilities, if any, should never be outsourced even if partners can deliver faster?

6. If pilot results are mixed after two quarters, do you tighten governance and narrow focus, or broaden experimentation to learn faster? What organizational signal does each choice send?
