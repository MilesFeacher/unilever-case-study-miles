## 1. Industry Economics, Market Size, and Key Trends

### 1.1 Economics of Global Consumer Goods in This Case Context

This case sits inside the global fast-moving consumer goods (FMCG) economy, where profit pools are shaped by scale, brand strength, channel power, and execution speed rather than by one-time product breakthroughs. In practical terms, economics are driven by:
- Gross margin discipline in categories with heavy promotional pressure.
- Distribution and logistics efficiency across fragmented retail channels.
- Demand generation effectiveness, especially where digital discovery influences purchase.
- Productivity programs that fund reinvestment while protecting earnings quality.

For this case, the most reliable market-size anchor is Unilever's audited FY2025 business scale:
- Turnover: EUR 50.5 billion.
- Underlying operating profit: EUR 10.1 billion.
- Free cash flow: EUR 5.9 billion (100% cash conversion).

These figures establish a company-scale market context: even small percentage shifts in conversion, margin, or logistics productivity can create material value at enterprise level.

### 1.2 Key Trends Relevant to the Decision

1. AI-mediated consumer discovery.
Consumer journeys are increasingly influenced by algorithmic ranking, conversational interfaces, and personalized recommendation systems. This shifts competitive advantage toward faster content adaptation, stronger data integration, and tighter brand-to-commerce feedback loops.

2. Convergence of marketing and operations.
Historically, demand generation and supply execution were managed in parallel. In an AI-first model, they become interdependent. If discovery accelerates without fulfillment reliability, growth leaks. If operations optimize without demand agility, assets underperform.

3. Platform concentration with capability differentiation.
Large CPG firms are standardizing on fewer cloud/data platforms while trying to preserve brand-level and category-level differentiation. The strategic challenge is deciding what should be common (data, governance, workflows) and what should remain unique (brand voice, innovation priorities, local execution patterns).

4. Productivity-funded transformation.
The EUR 800 million productivity program (on track for 2026 completion per transcript) is not just a cost story. It is a transformation-financing mechanism: savings can be reinvested into technology, data, media, and operating model redesign.

## 2. Competitive Landscape

### 2.1 Competitor Archetypes

In this case's evidence base, peers cluster into several strategic patterns:

1. Operations-led AI transformation.
Competitors emphasizing manufacturing/supply optimization and digital industrial execution.

2. Marketing and commerce AI acceleration.
Competitors emphasizing demand generation, personalization, creative at scale, and digital shelf performance.

3. Core-platform modernization.
Competitors emphasizing ERP/cloud-core simplification and enterprise process harmonization as the base for later AI scale.

Unilever's position in this case appears hybrid: it has tangible operations-side AI evidence (Lighthouse metrics) and explicit demand-generation ambitions (agentic shopping language, AI-enabled marketing production via Sketch Pro), but still faces integration decisions on sequencing and governance.

Third-party coverage reinforces that reading. Computer Weekly and Consumer Goods Technology described the Google Cloud move as a five-year shift toward a consolidated AI-first digital backbone, while FT partner content connected Google Cloud to Unilever's supply-chain visibility and sustainability use cases.

### 2.2 Practical Competitive Pressure in This Case

The pressure is not only "who has better AI." It is who can:
- Turn AI experiments into repeatable business outcomes.
- Scale use cases across regions without exploding complexity.
- Protect governance quality while moving faster than category growth rates.

In that sense, Unilever competes simultaneously on:
- Commercial speed (campaign-to-conversion cycle time).
- Operating reliability (fulfillment, quality, cost).
- Organizational coherence (shared standards and execution discipline).

The external-source mix matters here because it shows the partnership is being interpreted by independent outlets as an operating-model change, not just a vendor announcement.

## 3. Relevant Technology Concepts and Frameworks

### 3.1 Core Concepts Used in the Case

1. Agentic commerce.
A model in which AI agents assist or automate parts of shopping and decision processes, often across discovery, recommendation, and transaction flows. In this case, this is verified as strategic intent language; broad scaled deployment remains a caveated point.

2. AI-first foundation.
An architecture approach where enterprise data, workflows, and decision systems are designed assuming AI integration by default rather than as add-ons.

3. Factory-to-consumer distribution model.
A direct operational model connecting production and e-commerce fulfillment with high automation and digital orchestration.

4. Digital twin.
A virtual representation of physical operations used to optimize variables such as water use, energy, throughput, and quality.

5. Centralized governance with federated execution.
A management model where standards, controls, and architecture are set centrally, while business units retain execution responsibility inside clear guardrails.

### 3.2 Decision Framework for Sam Kini (Case-Use Framework)

A practical three-layer decision framework for this case:

Layer 1: Value layer (What outcome first?)
- Growth lift
- Margin expansion
- Cycle-time improvement
- Risk reduction

Layer 2: Capability layer (What must exist?)
- Data quality and interoperability
- Workflow instrumentation
- Model governance
- Talent and operating routines

Layer 3: Scale layer (How to roll out?)
- Commercialization-first
- Platform-first
- Federated dual-track with stage gates

The teaching tension is that these layers are interdependent but time-constrained. Choosing one "first move" always means accepting a second-order risk.

## 4. Glossary of Technical and Strategic Terms

- Agentic AI: AI systems designed to plan and execute multi-step tasks with limited human intervention within defined constraints.
- Agentic commerce: Use of agentic AI in shopping/discovery environments to influence or automate parts of purchase journeys.
- AI-first foundation: Enterprise architecture designed with AI as a default capability across systems and workflows.
- Cash conversion: Share of accounting earnings converted into free cash flow over a period.
- D-commerce: Digitally mediated commerce channels, including direct and platform-enabled pathways.
- Digital twin: Virtual model of a physical asset/process used for simulation and optimization.
- Federated model: Organizational setup combining central standards with local execution autonomy.
- Gross margin expansion: Improvement in gross profit as a percentage of turnover, often via mix, pricing, and productivity.
- Interoperability: Ability of systems, data, and workflows to work together reliably across platforms and functions.
- Lighthouse (WEF): A site recognized by the World Economic Forum for advanced, scaled use of Fourth Industrial Revolution technologies.
- Operating model: The way an organization structures roles, governance, processes, and decision rights to deliver strategy.
- Portfolio simplification: Reducing complexity in brands/categories/assets to improve focus and execution quality.
- Stage gate: Predefined decision checkpoint used to approve, pause, or stop a program based on evidence.
- Technical debt: Future cost/risk created by short-term technical decisions that reduce long-term maintainability or scalability.
