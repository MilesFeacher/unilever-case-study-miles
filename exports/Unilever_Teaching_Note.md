## Case Synopsis

In February 2026, Sam Kini, Chief Digital & Technology Officer at Unilever, faced a sequencing decision that had become common across large enterprises by the mid-2020s: **How should a $50B+ CPG company enter the AI-enabled commerce era?**

Unilever was financially strong (€50.5B turnover, €5.9B free cash flow) but strategically pressured. Consumer shopping behavior was shifting toward conversational and agentic experiences. Competitors were announcing AI commerce partnerships. Internally, dozens of AI projects were underway—some promising, some redundant, many risky in aggregate. The question was not whether to invest in AI; it was where to push first, with enough scale to matter and enough governance to avoid enterprise fragmentation.

Kini had to recommend one of three paths:

1. **Commercialization-first sprint**: Visible wins, marketing content acceleration, brand demand generation
2. **Platform-first consolidation**: Shared infrastructure, data standardization, governance rigor (slower initial ROI)
3. **Federated dual-track model**: Both tracks proceeding with strict stage gates and regular integration checkpoints

Each choice embodied different risk profiles: speed vs. stability, visible impact vs. sustainable architecture, centralization vs. resilience. None was objectively "right." The steering meeting would happen in a week.

This case invites students to reason through organizational sequencing under uncertainty, with incomplete information about competitive moves, technical feasibility, and organizational capacity. It is grounded in real financial data, actual leadership statements, and disclosed investment patterns from Unilever's 2024-2026 filings and announcements.

---

## Learning Objectives

After discussing this case, MBA students should be able to:

1. **Diagnose sequencing dilemmas in large-scale transformation** — Recognize the structural tension between speed-to-market and architectural soundness, and articulate the specific cost each choice extracts

2. **Make capital allocation decisions with incomplete information** — Build a defensible argument for phasing and prioritization when multiple paths are strategically plausible and data is mixed

3. **Design organizational boundaries around centralization and autonomy** — Specify which decisions must be centralized for strategy coherence and which must stay distributed for execution speed, with explicit rationales

4. **Communicate ambition without over-claiming capability** — Distinguish between honest signaling and overselling in competitive markets where narrative speed matters as much as operational reality

5. **Navigate value-tradeoff frameworks** — Move beyond "both/and" thinking to acknowledge genuine zero-sum choices: speed vs. stability, breadth vs. depth, proprietary vs. partner-enabled

## Suggested Pre-Class Assignment

**For discussion preparation (distribute 1 week before class):**

Read the case study (Main Case document, ~7 pages) and complete this worksheet before class. This ensures students arrive with initial positions they must then defend and refine in discussion.

### Assignment: Your First Move

In the role of Sam Kini, respond to each question in 2-3 sentences. Be specific; avoid hedging language like "it depends" or "both are important."

**Question 1:** You can recommend only one first move: commercialization-first, platform-first, or federated dual-track. Which do you choose and why?

**Question 2:** Name the top three **centralized decisions** that must happen now to make your choice work. For each one, explain why it cannot stay distributed.

**Question 3:** What is the ONE headline success metric in 12 months that will define whether your choice was right? Be prepared to defend the tradeoff this metric hides.

**Question 4:** What is the biggest risk you are accepting with your choice? How would you know in Q2 2026 if that risk had materialized?

## Discussion Guide

**Target session length:** 75-90 minutes  
**Format:** Cold call from first 3 questions; then open discussion

---

### Opening: The Monday Morning Question (5-7 minutes)

**Read aloud (or have a student read) this excerpt from the case:**

> "The question in front of Kini, Unilever's Chief Digital and Technology Officer, was not whether the company should embrace AI. That argument had already been won in principle. The harder question was sequencing in practice. Where should Unilever push hard first, with enough scale to matter and enough control to avoid enterprise fragmentation?"

**Opening question:** "Before we dig into the case, turn to the person next to you: Why is sequencing *harder* than the decision to invest?"

**Expected responses:** 
- "Because you can't do everything at once"
- "Because the first choice locks in organizational structure"
- "Because speed and stability are in tension"
- "Because you don't know which will pay off first"

**Purpose of this exchange:** Surface the fact that most executives have already made the investment decision; the real tension is about *order*. This frames the case as a sequencing problem, not an investment-or-not problem.

**Transition:** "That tension between speed and architecture, between visible wins and sustainable foundations, is exactly what Kini faces. Let's look at why."

---

### Section 1: Context and Pressure (12-15 minutes)

**Case sections:** 1-4 (Monday Morning Question through Leadership Context)

**Focus:** Build consensus about the constraints Kini is operating within. This section should establish the business reality, not debate the solutions.

**Key framing question (cold call):** "Let's start with someone from finance or operations: If you were looking at Unilever's FY2025 numbers, what would tell you this company has *capacity* to invest in AI transformation? What would tell you it's under *pressure* to move fast?"

**Expected flow:**
- Students cite financials: €50.5B turnover, €5.9B FCF, 100% cash conversion, €6B shareholder returns
- Establish: "Financially healthy but not cash-flush for massive experimentation"
- Push back: "But if they have €5.9B FCF, why can't they just invest everywhere?"
- Student should recognize: "Because scale matters—€5.9B is a lot, but it has to cover core operations, shareholder returns, and risky AI pilots simultaneously"
- Board plan (draw): 
  ```
  FINANCIAL REALITY
  Strong | Under Pressure
  €5.9B FCF | €50.5B turnover (-3.8% YoY)
  100% cash conversion | Margin pressure
  €6B shareholder returns | Competitive velocity
  ```

**Second focus question:** "Here's a sentence from the case: 'If she optimized for speed, she could produce visible commercial wins and support leadership's growth narrative. If she optimized for architecture and governance first, she could reduce long-term risk but appear slow in a market where competitors announced new AI moves almost monthly.' Why is this a real tension? Why can't you do both?"

**Expected responses:**
- "You can't do both because you have limited engineering and capital resources"
- "Leadership and the board want both, but they don't understand the sequencing tradeoff"
- "Speed early signals weakness in architecture later"
- "Architecture now means looking slow while competitors move"

**Purpose:** Establish that *all three paths are reasonable given the constraints*. This prevents students from dismissing options too quickly. The goal is to make them wrestle with sequencing intentionality, not dismiss options as obviously wrong.

**Transition:** "Kini's past track record is important here. She comes into this with credibility on infrastructure and transformation. That changes her options."

---

### Section 2: Leadership Credibility and Past Execution (10-12 minutes)

**Case section:** 2 (A Leader Built For Change)

**Focus:** Discuss what makes Kini credible to the leadership team and board. This matters because her recommendation will carry weight precisely because of her history.

**Key question (open):** "What signals credibility to a board when a CDTO recommends a sequencing choice? What would make you skeptical?"

**Expected responses:**
- "Past success on similar transformations (cloud, SAP consolidation)"
- "Ability to articulate tradeoffs without hedging"
- "Track record of finishing projects, not abandoning them mid-way"
- "Clear about scope: knowing what fits in 'CDTO-led' vs. what needs business unit ownership"

**Point to make:** "Kini's credibility is that she's delivered on complex infrastructure work. She's not a 'move fast and break things' operator. That means her board will *listen* if she recommends platform-first, because they know she's not just being risk-averse—she's applying lessons from SAP consolidation and cloud migration. But it also means if she recommends commercialization-first, that choice has weight because she's not recommending it out of impatience."

**Board plan (add):**
```
KINI'S CREDIBILITY
Delivered infrastructure projects (SAP, Cloud)
Emphasized psychological safety & learning
Centralized architecture reduces long-term complexity
→ Board will take her sequencing recommendation seriously, 
  whether it's fast or slow
```

**Why this matters:** This prevents students from assuming Kini should automatically recommend the "safe" architecture-first option. Her credibility *enables* speed if she thinks speed is right.

**Transition:** "So Kini has credibility, the company is financially healthy but under pressure, and multiple paths are live. Now let's look at what's actually happening on the ground in terms of AI initiatives."

---

### Section 3: The Complexity of Existing Pilots (10-12 minutes)

**Case sections:** 5-6 (Evidence the Company Could Execute / Market Pressure)

**Focus:** Discuss the risk of unmanaged proliferation. This is where the case gets specific about why sequencing matters operationally, not just strategically.

**Key question (cold call on someone who has led a portfolio or program):** "The case mentions dozens of AI projects already running. What's the risk of 'many successful pilots, no shared operating system'? Why is that worse than 'a few pilots, slow progress'?"

**Expected responses:**
- "Each pilot learns something, but they don't integrate, so you rebuild capabilities repeatedly"
- "You end up with fragmented data, different vendors, incompatible models"
- "Talent gets spread thin across too many initiatives, burning out early"
- "You look productive but you're not actually building company-wide capability"

**Point to make:** "That's the core sequencing problem. It's not that pilots are bad; it's that *uncoordinated pilots at this scale consume the same resources as a coordinated program, but produce less runway insight*. Kini has to impose a sequencing decision specifically to prevent proliferation from becoming fragmentation."

**Specific case fact to highlight:** "The case says she needs to 'push hard first, with enough scale to matter and enough control to avoid enterprise fragmentation.' This is the operational constraint. It's not 'avoid all risk'—it's avoid the specific risk of 80 unconnected initiatives all competing for the same data engineers and governance bandwidth."

**Board plan (add):**
```
EXISTING PILOT RISK
Many initiatives ≠ Shared operating system
Proliferation → Resource fragmentation → Organizational drag
Need: Sequencing that consolidates learning from pilots 
      without killing local ownership
```

**Transition:** "This is where the three options on the table really diverge. Let's look at each one."

---

### Section 4: The Three Paths (20-25 minutes)

**Case section:** 7 (What Exactly Is The Decision?)

**Focus:** For each of the three options, have students articulate the theory of change and the earliest "decision point" that would reveal if they were right or wrong. Do not let them dismiss any option as obviously inferior.

**Setup question (cold call):** "Walk me through the theory of change for commercialization-first. If I'm the CFO, why should I believe this approach leads to sustainable capability in 18 months, not just a sugar rush?"

**Expected responses:**
- "Because early commercial wins give you permission and budget for the hard infrastructure work later"
- "Because you learn from customers what architecture actually needs to support, so you don't over-engineer"
- "Because team morale and momentum from fast wins accelerates the platform build"

**Push back:** "But what's the risk? What if after 6 months of fast wins, the technical debt is so high that the infrastructure work takes 2 years, not 6 months?"

**Student might say:** "That's why you need some governance guardrails even in commercialization-first mode."

**Respond:** "Exactly. So the real question is not 'commercialization vs. platform' in an absolute sense; it's 'what's the minimum governance you need early, and what's the maximum speed you can sustain without collapsing later?'"

**Then do the same for platform-first:**

**Setup:** "Now flip it. I'm a business unit head with quarterly revenue targets. Walk me through why I should accept a platform-first approach when my competitors are launching agentic commerce pilots in Q2 2026."

**Expected responses:**
- "Because building shared data infrastructure first means every pilot you run after month 6 goes faster and pulls higher quality insights"
- "Because you avoid the trap of rebuilding the same data pipeline 10 times"
- "Because governance and data standards now prevent future rework"

**Push back:** "But what if your platform investment takes 9 months instead of 6, and competitors have already locked in customer habit by then? How do you recover?"

**Student might say:** "You have to move faster on the platform work, or you have to accept that you're ceding first-mover advantage."

**Respond:** "That's the real tradeoff. There's a speed limit on how fast you can do platform work *correctly*. If you compress it too much, you recreate the fragmentation problem. So the question becomes: given your talent constraints, what's the fastest you can build platform infrastructure *and have it actually be adopted by the business units*?"

**Then the federated dual-track:**

**Setup:** "And the middle path: federated dual-track with stage gates. Why would Kini recommend this? Who does this help, and who does it hurt?"

**Expected responses:**
- "It helps you preserve autonomy in business units that have viable near-term opportunities"
- "It lets you build platform infrastructure in parallel with early commercialization"
- "It hurts because you're running two expensive tracks, and if they diverge, you have to kill the duplicate work"

**Board plan (draw a 2x2 or timeline):**
```
SEQUENCING OPTIONS

                SPEED          RISK
Commercialization-first:  High          Debt + fragmentation
Platform-first:           Low           First-mover disadvantage  
Federated dual-track:     Medium        Resource split + divergence

Decision point (all paths): Q2 2026 (6 months in)
Pilot results = signal for phase 2 scope
```

**Critical moment in discussion:** Have students vote (show of hands) for each option. Then ask the dissenters: "What would have to be true about the company's current capabilities or market timing for your choice to beat the majority vote?"

**Expected responses will expose assumptions:**
- "Commercialization-first voters assume platform work can be done *faster* later than most people predict"
- "Platform-first voters assume you *can afford* to cede market share for 12 months"
- "Federated voters assume you have the engineering depth to staff two parallel programs"

**Purpose:** This reveals that the choice depends on answers to three unresolved questions:
1. How fast can you actually build platform infrastructure with high adoption?
2. How much first-mover advantage does agentic commerce actually confer?
3. What is your true talent constraint: engineering, capital, or management attention?

**Transition:** "Notice that reasonable people disagree on these. That's not a bug in the case—it's the point. Kini doesn't have perfect information either."

---

### Section 5: Centralization vs. Decentralization (10-12 minutes)

**Case section:** 8 (Strategic Tensions Without Clean Answers)

**Focus:** Discuss the organizational design implications of the sequencing choice. This is where strategy becomes structure.

**Key question (cold call on an operations or org design person):** "Whichever path Kini chooses, she has to decide what stays centralized and what stays distributed. Why does sequencing choice matter for this organizational boundary?"

**Expected responses:**
- "If you go commercialization-first, you can leave vendor selection distributed because speed matters more than standardization"
- "If you go platform-first, you must centralize data standards, or the platform won't work"
- "If you go federated, you need centralized governance but distributed execution"

**Point to make:** "The sequencing choice is *not* actually separable from the organizational design. If Kini recommends commercialization-first, she's implicitly saying 'I will centralize less at the start and coordinate integration points later.' If she recommends platform-first, she's saying 'I will centralize heavily now and loosen it later.' The middle path is the riskiest because it requires sustained coordination without clear authority structure."

**Specific question (open):** "Name three decisions that *must* be centralized in any of these three paths, and two decisions that *should* stay in business units. Be specific."

**Expected responses:**
- *Centralized:* Data architecture, model governance, vendor landscape (avoid 10 different LLM contracts), customer data privacy, measurement standards
- *Distributed:* How marketing uses AI content generation, which customer segment to target first, specific use-case prioritization, local campaign testing

**Board plan (add):**
```
ORGANIZATIONAL DESIGN FOLLOWS SEQUENCING

Centralized (all paths):     Decentralized (all paths):
- Data architecture          - Use-case prioritization
- Model governance           - Local campaign design
- Vendor rationalization     - Segment targeting
- Privacy/compliance         - Testing velocity
- Measurement framework
```

**Transition:** "This brings us to the communication challenge. How does Kini talk about this to the board, to competitors, and to the market without over-claiming what will actually happen?"

---

### Section 6: Signaling vs. Overselling (8-10 minutes)

**Case section:** 8 (Strategic Tensions) and cold-call question from main case

**Focus:** This is the narrative/communications challenge embedded in the strategy work. It's easy to miss, so make it explicit.

**Key question (cold call from marketing or comms):** "The case includes this question: 'How should leadership communicate agentic-commerce ambition externally without over-claiming operational maturity?' Why is this a real problem for a company like Unilever?"

**Expected responses:**
- "Because competitors and analysts will assume you have more capability than you actually do"
- "Because if you announce a commitment to customers and then miss, you damage credibility"
- "Because your supply chain partners and retailers will want to integrate with your AI systems before you're ready"
- "Because activists and critics will hold you to a higher standard if you've oversold"

**Specific tension:** "So here's the dilemma: If you describe your AI strategy as 'two-year platform build,' you're honest about sequencing, but it sounds slow and defensive. If you describe it as 'agentic commerce at scale by end of 2026,' you're matching competitor rhetoric, but you're claiming capability you might not have in 12 months. Where's the line?"

**Challenge to students:** "Write one sentence that Unilever could truthfully say about AI and commerce that is *neither* defensive nor over-claiming."

**Expected responses might include:**
- "We are partnering with [vendor] to accelerate consumer discovery in agentic environments, with initial pilots in [region] by [date]."
- "We are building the data infrastructure to power next-generation consumer experiences while maintaining privacy and brand safety."
- "We are moving beyond marketing content to consumer interaction architecture, with rigorous stage gates to ensure readiness."

**Point to make:** "The language you choose reveals how much governance you've actually done. Specific pilots and dates signal real commitment. Vague language about 'transformation' signals either under-planning or over-claiming. Kini has to choose rhetoric that aligns with her chosen path. And the board will hold her to it."

**Transition:** "We're almost there. Let's talk about the decision moment itself."

---

### Closing: Your Recommendation (8-10 minutes)

**Scenario return to the cold-call framework:**

**Setup:** "It's Wednesday morning. Kini's steering meeting is Thursday. She's been asked to prepare a 10-minute recommendation with one slide. Here's what the slide will say:"

*Board Plan (reveal final state):*
```
SAM KINI'S RECOMMENDATION (One Slide)

PATH: [Commercialization-first / Platform-first / Federated dual-track]

12-MONTH SUCCESS METRIC: [Specific KPI]

TOP 3 CENTRALIZED DECISIONS:
1. [Decision]
2. [Decision]
3. [Decision]

BIGGEST RISK I'M ACCEPTING: [Risk]

Q2 2026 GO/NO-GO CHECKPOINT: [Specific milestone]
```

**Cold call:** "You're the CFO. Which of Kini's three options would you want to hear, and which would make you nervous? Why?"

**Then:** "You're a business unit head with a near-term campaign opportunity. Which option helps you, and which constrains you?"

**Then:** "You're the Chief Risk Officer. Which option worries you most, and why?"

**Critical moment:** Have students vote again (second vote, now that they've heard the full discussion). Compare to the first vote. What changed? What remained consistent?

**Synthesis:** "Notice that most people didn't change their core choice, but their *reasoning* probably got more sophisticated. You probably moved from 'platform-first is obviously safer' to 'platform-first is right *if* the company can sustain a 12-month slow phase without board pressure to show faster results.' That difference—from principle to conditional reasoning—is what strategic thinking looks like."

**Final question (open, no cold call):** "If you were Kini, what would you do differently than the case suggests? What information would you want that she doesn't have?"

**This surfaces uncertainties the case deliberately leaves open:**
- Real market adoption curves for agentic commerce (not known in Feb 2026)
- Competitor roadmaps (partially known, incompletely known)
- Internal organizational capacity for parallel workstreams (known to Kini, not to us as case readers)

**Transition to close:** "That uncertainty is the point. Strategic decisions are made with incomplete information. What matters is articulating your assumptions clearly enough that:
1. The board can disagree with your assumptions, not your logic
2. You can revise early if assumptions prove wrong
3. Others can execute with confidence because they know what you're betting on"

## Analysis Framework: The Sequencing Choice Matrix

This framework helps structure student thinking and can be used for group work or individual assessments.

### Framework: Speed vs. Sustainability (2x2)

```
                          PLATFORM READINESS
                    Low              High
          ┌──────────────────────────────────┐
          │  Option A          │   Option C   │
    HIGH  │  Commercialization │  Federated   │
          │  First             │  Dual-Track  │
SPEED     ├──────────────────────────────────┤
          │  Option B          │              │
    LOW   │  Platform-First    │  (N/A)       │
          │                    │              │
          └──────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Interpretation:**
- **Option A (Commercialization-first):** High speed, low platform readiness. Bet: Can build platform quickly later.
- **Option B (Platform-first):** Low speed, builds platform readiness. Bet: Market can wait, and foundation enables sustainable growth.
- **Option C (Federated):** Medium speed, medium platform readiness (intentionally). Bet: Can maintain two workstreams and integrate later.

**Discussion prompt:** "For each option, what would have to go *wrong* for Kini to regret the choice in Q4 2026?"

## Potential Student Positions

| Position | Core Argument | Counter | Resolution Path |
|----------|---|---|---|
| **Commercialization-first advocate** | "Speed and wins build momentum and board support for the hard infrastructure work later. Platform work is never 'done,' so starting slow leaves you behind competitors." | "You're assuming you can compress platform work into 3 months in late 2026 without quality loss. What if you can't? You're locked into fragmented architecture." | Concede that platform work has a minimum duration, but argue that clear Q2 checkpoint enables early course correction. |
| **Platform-first advocate** | "Unilever is not Amazon. You don't win on speed; you win on brand trust and reliability. A broken or fragmented AI system damages brand more than a slow one." | "But CPG innovation cycles are measured in quarters, not years. By the time your platform is ready, agentic commerce will have evolved, and your architecture will be outdated anyway." | Argue that Unilever's brand *is* a competitive advantage, so platform reliability matters more than being first. Concede market timing risk. |
| **Federated dual-track advocate** | "Why choose? If you have the engineering depth, running both tracks lets you learn from both and integrate the best of each." | "You're almost certainly underestimating the coordination overhead. Most failures of dual-track programs happen because neither track moves fast enough to satisfy the board." | Concede that federated requires sustained governance, but argue that a clear stage gate (Q2 checkpoint) makes divergence explicit and prevents waste. |
| **"We don't have enough info" skeptic** | "The case doesn't tell us competitive roadmaps, customer preferences, or real technical constraints. We're deciding on narrative, not facts." | "That's actually the point. Strategic decisions happen with incomplete info. If you wait for perfect info, you've already lost." | Acknowledge uncertainty while arguing that the three options have *different* uncertainties. Build a decision tree that maps each option to the uncertainties it *reduces*. |

**How to use this table:** When a student makes an argument, locate it in the Position column and use the Counter to push back. Model the Resolution Path to show how reasonable people can acknowledge counterarguments and still maintain their position—because they've been explicit about their assumptions.

## Assessment Options

### Option 1: Individual Write-Up (2,000-2,500 words)

**Assignment:**

> You are a management consultant advising Kini. Write a memo recommending one of the three sequencing paths.
> 
> **Structure:**
> - **Executive Summary (200 words):** Your recommendation and the core rationale in plain language
> - **Context (400 words):** Why the decision matters, what risks Unilever faces, what Kini's constraints are
> - **Your Choice and Why (600 words):** Lay out the case for your recommendation vs. the alternatives
> - **What Success Looks Like (400 words):** Specific 12-month metric, top 3 centralized decisions, biggest risk you're accepting
> - **The Decision Checkpoint (300 words):** What will you measure in Q2 2026 that will tell you if your choice was right or wrong? If the checkpoint reveals risk, what's your pivot plan?
> 
> **Grading rubric:**
> - Does the memo acknowledge legitimate counterarguments? (20 pts)
> - Is the 12-month metric specific and defensible? (20 pts)
> - Are the top 3 centralized decisions clear and justified? (20 pts)
> - Does the checkpoint scenario reveal genuine decision criteria (not just performance reporting)? (20 pts)
> - Is the writing clear enough that Kini could present this to a board? (20 pts)

**Strong responses will:**
- Acknowledge the tension between speed and sustainability explicitly, not pretend it doesn't exist
- Name specific risks (not vague risks like "execution risk")
- Show that they've thought through organizational design implications of their choice
- Include a checkpoint that would genuinely force a pivot, not just measure progress

### Option 2: Group Presentation (15 minutes + 10-minute Q&A)

**Assignment:**

> As a 4-person executive advisory team, prepare a recommendation for Kini.
> 
> **Your team roles:**
> - 1 person: Business unit leader (responsible for near-term revenue)
> - 1 person: Chief Technology Officer (responsible for architecture)
> - 1 person: Chief People/Organizational Officer (responsible for culture and talent)
> - 1 person: CFO (responsible for capital allocation)
> 
> **Presentation structure (15 minutes total):**
> - **Opening (2 min):** State your recommendation
> - **Business case (3 min):** What this choice enables commercially in 12 months
> - **Technical feasibility (3 min):** Why this is achievable given Unilever's technical depth
> - **Organizational design (3 min):** How you'll structure the work—who leads, who's distributed, what governance
> - **Risk and checkpoint (4 min):** Your biggest bet, what could go wrong, what Q2 2026 checkpoint reveals
> 
> **Q&A (10 minutes):** Be prepared for the board to push back on:
> - Why this is faster/safer than the alternative paths
> - How you'd handle it if your assumptions prove wrong in Q2 2026
> - Why you centralized these three decisions and not others
> - How you'll communicate this externally without over-claiming

**Grading rubric:**
- Did all four roles speak (team communication)? (20 pts)
- Is the recommendation internally coherent (business logic aligns with tech feasibility aligns with org design)? (30 pts)
- Did the team anticipate board pushback and have credible answers? (20 pts)
- Is the checkpoint concrete enough to force a real pivot if needed? (20 pts)
- Would an actual board find this presentation professional and decision-ready? (10 pts)

**Strong responses will:**
- Show that all four roles agree on the sequencing *and* have thought through their piece
- Reveal that they've mapped organizational boundaries to the sequencing choice
- Include a checkpoint that trades off new learning against the cost of changing direction

### Option 3: Exam Question (Timed, 2 hours)

**Assignment:**

> Kini is asking three different groups for input. For each scenario below, write a 5-7 minute briefing (750-1,000 words) from that perspective. You must recommend a different path for each scenario, and your recommendations must be defensible *from that perspective*.
>
> **Scenario A: You are presenting to the CFO** (focused on capital efficiency and board optics)
> 
> **Scenario B: You are presenting to the business unit heads** (focused on near-term revenue and autonomy)
> 
> **Scenario C: You are presenting to the technical leadership team** (focused on architectural quality and talent retention)
> 
> For each scenario, name your recommended path, explain why it makes sense from that stakeholder's perspective, and acknowledge the biggest risk that stakeholder faces if you're wrong.

**Grading rubric:**
- Does each briefing reflect genuine stakeholder priorities (not caricatured)? (25 pts)
- Are the three recommendations strategically coherent even though they differ by stakeholder? (25 pts)
- Does each briefing acknowledge that trade-offs exist (not hide them)? (25 pts)
- Is the writing clear and concise enough that it could be used in an actual stakeholder conversation? (25 pts)

**Strong responses will:**
- Recognize that "the right answer" depends on whose perspective you're taking
- Show that the same facts lead to different recommendations based on different values and constraints
- Avoid treating this as "the CFO is right" or "the technologists are right"—instead show the internal logic of each perspective

## Teaching Notes: Common Classroom Moments

### Moment 1: "Just Do Both"

**What students often say:** "Why can't you just do platform-first AND commercialization at the same time? Hire more people."

**Why this happens:** Students often assume the constraint is capital, not talent or management attention. They're under-estimating the coordination cost of parallel work.

**How to respond:** "Let's test that assumption. Name the specific hiring: how many architects do you need for platform-first, and how many will you redeploy from ongoing core operations? If you keep both teams fully staffed, the CFO is going to ask: where does this money come from? Do we cut shareholder returns, or do we reduce other investment? Show me the p&l."

**The learning:** Strategic constraints are usually not single-factor. It's almost never "just hire more people." It's usually capacity, capital, *and* talent *and* management attention all constrained simultaneously. Once students recognize this, they stop proposing "both/and" solutions.

### Moment 2: "They're Not Communicating Clearly"

**What students often say:** "If Unilever is confused about their AI strategy, that's a communication problem. They should just articulate it better."

**Why this happens:** Students often assume that clarity of communication can overcome strategic tension. They underestimate that sometimes the tension is real, not just poorly explained.

**How to respond:** "The tension is structural, not communicational. Here's the question: do we commit to agentic commerce as a core business model, or do we treat it as a marketing tool? If it's core business, we need platform-first. If it's marketing, we can do commercialization-first. But the board hasn't decided that yet, and Kini's recommendation *will decide it*. So the lack of clarity is not a failure—it's a signal that the strategic choice hasn't been made. Kini is the person who gets to make it."

**The learning:** Sometimes muddiness is not a communication failure; it's a signal that the strategic choice hasn't been locked. Kini's job is to lock it.

### Moment 3: "Unilever Should Have Done This Years Ago"

**What students often say:** "This is a 2026 problem. Why didn't they start this in 2023 or 2024?"

**Why this happens:** Students often apply hindsight bias. They forget that in 2023-2024, the capabilities and market didn't yet exist at the scale they do in 2026.

**How to respond:** "Fair question. But in 2024, agentic commerce wasn't yet a proven business model. ChatGPT was 1 year old. The question then was not 'how do we lead agentic commerce' but 'is this even real or a hype cycle?' Kini's job now is to move because the market has made a choice—agentic commerce is real. But she couldn't have led into a business model that didn't yet exist. The timing of the decision follows the market maturation curve, not a failure of Unilever's planning."

**The learning:** Strategic timing matters. You move when the market is ready, not before. Kini is moving at the right moment.

### Moment 4: "What's the 'Right' Answer?"

**What students ask:** "So which path does Unilever actually choose?" or "What does the case say is right?"

**Why this happens:** Students are trained to find the "right answer" in case studies. They're uncomfortable with open-endedness.

**How to respond:** "The case doesn't resolve it because in February 2026, it's unresolved. If you want to know what actually happened, you can look up Unilever's 2026 earnings call (which is in the future as we're teaching this, but will be real by the time you graduate). The point of the case is not to predict what they did; it's to reason through what *you* would do and why. Can you articulate a defensible choice? Great. Can you acknowledge the tradeoff your choice accepts? Even better. Can you name the Q2 2026 checkpoint that would force you to reconsider? Perfect."

**The learning:** Strategic cases don't have "correct answers"—they have defensible reasoning. The goal is to move students from "what's the answer?" to "what's my reasoning, what am I betting on, and what would change my mind?"

## Extensions and Variations

### Extension 1: Competitive Context

**If students finish early or want deeper analysis:**

> Research the actual AI commerce moves made by Unilever competitors in 2025-2026 (P&G, Nestlé, Reckitt, etc.). Map their paths: Did they go commercialization-first, platform-first, or federated? What were the results reported by Q3 2026? How does this context change Kini's decision calculus?

**Purpose:** Moves the case from abstract reasoning to competitive intelligence. Shows how real market moves constrain individual choices.

### Extension 2: Technical Deep Dive

**If you have technical students or if time allows:**

> Take the platform-first path and design the actual architecture. What data infrastructure must exist by month 6 for pilots to work? What's the minimum viable platform? What decisions would fragment it? Build a technical decision tree that maps to the governance choices in the case.

**Purpose:** Translates "platform-first" from business language to engineering reality. Exposes the hidden technical assumptions in the case.

### Extension 3: International Dimension

**If your program has global context:**

> Unilever operates in 180+ markets. The case focuses on centralized decisions from London. What changes if you have to implement the same sequencing choice across radically different markets (e.g., North America vs. Southeast Asia vs. Africa)? Does federated dual-track become federated *multiple*-track? How does this change Kini's risk calculus?

**Purpose:** Moves from single-company decision to multi-geography execution. Reveals how centralization vs. decentralization looks different at scale.

## Facilitation Tips

1. **Cold call strategically:** Use cold calls early (Section 1) to build comfort with the material, then use open questions later (Sections 3-4) to deepen debate.

2. **Use the whiteboard as a thinking tool:** Don't pre-write the board plan. Build it as students speak. Let them see their reasoning become visual structure.

3. **When stuck:** Return to the constraint: "What's the decision being made here? What's the tradeoff?" Most off-topic discussions snap back to focus when you name the constraint explicitly.

4. **When consensus is too fast:** Be suspicious. If everyone agrees on a path by minute 30, you've probably let a reasonable counterargument go unstated. Cold call someone who would have disagreed: "I want to hear from someone who thinks that's the wrong choice. What would have to be true for your path to beat the majority choice?"

5. **At the end:** Don't wrap up with "so the answer is..." Instead, wrap up with "so *you* chose... and here's what that choice means for the next 12 months. The board's job now is to hold you accountable to that choice and the checkpoint you set." Leave the tension alive.

## Final Note: The Pedagogy

This teaching note is built around **Socratic questioning**, not lecture. The goal is to move students from "I would pick X because it's safer/faster" to "I would pick X because I'm accepting Y risk and betting that Z will be true, and here's my Q2 2026 checkpoint to test that assumption."

That intellectual move—from stated preference to reasoned choice to testable assumption—is what separates opinion from strategy. Everything in this note (the board plan, the discussion questions, the framework, the assessment options) is designed to push students through that sequence.

The case is intentionally unresolved. That's not a flaw—that's the feature.
